From Tokens to Policy:
Causal and Interpretable Heterogeneous
Treatment Effects Identification

Riccardo Cadei1,  Frank Otchere2,  Nyasha Tirivayi2,  Gustavo A. Tagliaferro2,  Falco J. Bargagli-Stoffi3,  Francesco Locatello1
1ISTA   2UNICEF   3UCLA
arXiv Code BibTeX
Heterogeneous Treatment Effect (HTE) identification is crucial to explain the impact of an intervention and optimize our policies accordingly. Existing approaches trade expressivity for interpretability, but as long as some active heterogeneity drivers are unmeasured, both ends of this spectrum allow for spurious HTE characterization with no causal reading. We focus on controlled experiments and argue that the oracle causal characterization via the direct effect modifiers is now within reach, thanks to (i) more extensive pre-treatment measurements, e.g., multi-modal, and (ii) representation learning pipelines to scale their analyses. We re-frame HTE identification as a Markov-blanket discovery problem on a sufficient and aligned pre-treatment representation, and introduce Neural EXposure Interaction Search (NEXIS), an iterative procedure with provable and empirically validated consistent selection. We deploy NEXIS on two anti-poverty programs in Africa, augmenting each with satellite imagery capturing previously unmeasured environmental effect modifiers, leading to novel interpretable and prescriptive guidelines to optimize the programs’ next iterations.

Introduction

TL;DR We introduce NEXIS, a principled iterative procedure over sufficient and interpretable pre-treatment representations to identify the causal characterization of treatment effect heterogeneity — directly answering “what if, or how, should we modify our treatment assignment policy?”

Contributions

  • Causal and interpretable HTE identification
    Targeting causal and human-interpretable HTE identification, formulated as a Markov-blanket discovery problem over a sufficient and aligned pre-treatment representation. This is the first procedure to identify a causal and interpretable HTE characterization from complex observations, translating raw measurements end-to-end into practical guidelines for policy adaptation.
  • NEXIS — a principled procedure with recovery guarantees
    A novel forward-backward procedure based on conditional effect-equivalence testing, with high-probability recovery guarantees (Theorem 1). Extensive empirical validation and ablations on semi-synthetic experiments provide practical guidelines for different data regimes, covering test choice, multiple-testing correction, spectral-gap threshold, and backward elimination.
  • Two anti-poverty program deployments
    Deploying NEXIS on the Youth Opportunities Program (Uganda) and LEAP 1000 (Ghana), augmenting each with novel satellite imagery and capturing previously ignored environmental factors. The results provide concrete, prescriptive guidelines for the next iterations of these programs.

Experimental Power Paradox

Effect Modifier

A variable is an effect modifier if its value is associated with how much a unit benefits from the treatment. This is a purely statistical notion: it can be detected by testing whether the variable predicts treatment-effect heterogeneity, independently of all other covariates.

Direct Effect Modifier (Interactor)

A variable is a direct effect modifier if it causally drives the heterogeneity — its effect on the treatment response remains after conditioning on every other candidate. Every interactor is an effect modifier, but not vice versa.

Example: Consider a cash transfer program (LEAP 1000) targeting mothers in Northern Ghana, where the outcome is monthly household expenditure on infant care. Market presence is the latent direct modifier — communities near markets can convert the cash into goods for the newborn more effectively. Road density, visible from satellite imagery, correlates with market presence and surfaces as an effect modifier, but only by common cause. A naive interpretation suggests building roads to amplify the program effect; the actual driver is market presence, so a road built where no market exists leaves the effect unchanged.

Experimental Power Paradox

Marginal screening tests each variable independently and cannot distinguish effect modifiers from interactors: as experimental power grows, it flags every variable correlated with the treatment effect — including non-causal proxies like road density. More data makes spurious discoveries more significant, not fewer. NEXIS conditions on competing candidates and recovers only the minimal set of true interactors.

Method

From Tokens to Policy — a four-step cycle

[ Pipeline animation — coming soon ]
  1. Run a controlled experiment. Observe that the treatment effect varies across units. The key question: which pre-treatment features actually cause that variation — the direct effect modifiers Wdir?
  2. Represent complex measurements via Foundation Model + SAE. Pre-treatment observations (satellite imagery, medical imaging, …) are encoded by a frozen foundation model, then decomposed by a learned Sparse Autoencoder into a dictionary of interpretable atoms. Representation Sufficiency (SAE reconstruction) and Principal Alignment (one neuron per latent modifier) are the two key desiderata.
  3. Identify the direct effect modifiers with NEXIS. NEXIS runs a forward–backward Markov-blanket search over all candidate neurons, testing conditional CATE-equivalence at each step with Bonferroni-gated p-values. It returns the minimal and sufficient set S* with FWER ≤ α. Each selected neuron is automatically labelled by a VLM (Qwen2.5-VL-72B) contrasting top- vs. bottom-activating tiles.
    [ NEXIS forward–backward search animation — coming soon ]
  4. Update policy accordingly. The output is a causal and interpretable characterisation of who benefits most, directly actionable for program adaptation.

Application 1: Youth Opportunities Program (Uganda)

Program

The Youth Opportunities Program (YOP) was a randomised cash-grant experiment conducted in post-conflict Northern Uganda in 2008. The government awarded one-time grants (~$7,500 USD) to randomly selected groups of young adults, enabling them to pursue skilled artisan or business activity. The program targeted communities in five linguistic sub-regions severely affected by the LRA conflict.

Data

The trial enrolled 439 groups across 331 communities (422 geo-coded sites), with individual-level baseline and endline surveys covering labour-market outcomes (skilled employment, business assets) and pre-treatment demographic covariates (age, education, household composition, linguistic group).

Satellite extension

We extend the trial with Landsat-7 imagery (2005–2007), embedded via Prithvi-EO and summarised by a 1,024-atom SAE, adding 146 active SAE atoms and spectral indices to the demographic covariates — 170 total NEXIS candidates. The map below shows trial communities colored by linguistic group or treatment assignment.

Application 2: Livelihood Empowerment Against Poverty (Ghana)

Program

The Livelihood Empowerment Against Poverty 1000 (LEAP 1000) is Ghana's national unconditional cash transfer program, targeting pregnant women and mothers of children under one year of age in the country's poorest districts. A randomised evaluation was conducted in Northern Ghana between 2015 and 2017, measuring the program's impact on household consumption and welfare.

Data

The trial enrolled 2,331 households in 162 communities across the NorthEast, Northern, Savannah, and Upper East regions. Baseline (2015) and endline (2017) surveys provide household-level consumption outcomes and 24 pre-treatment covariates (demographics, asset ownership, household composition). The overall difference-in-differences ATE is +7.35 GH₡/month.

Satellite extension

We extend the trial with Landsat-8 2015 imagery, embedded via Prithvi-EO and summarised by a 4,096-atom SAE, combined with the 24 survey covariates — 155 total NEXIS candidates. The map below shows GPS centroids of the 162 trial communities, colored by study region.
