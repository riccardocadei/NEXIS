From Tokens to Policy:
Causal and Interpretable Heterogeneous
Treatment Effects Identification

Riccardo Cadei1,  Frank Otchere2,  Nyasha Tirivayi2,  Gustavo Angeles Tagliaferro2,  Falco J. Bargagli-Stoffi3,  Francesco Locatello1
1ISTA   2UNICEF   3UCLA
arXiv GitHub BibTeX
Heterogeneous Treatment Effect (HTE) identification is crucial to explain the impact of an intervention and optimize our policies accordingly. Existing approaches trade expressivity for interpretability, but as long as some active heterogeneity drivers are unmeasured, both ends of this spectrum allow for spurious HTE characterization with no causal reading. We focus on controlled experiments and argue that the oracle causal characterization via the direct effect modifiers is now within reach, thanks to (i) more extensive pre-treatment measurements, e.g., multi-modal, and (ii) representation learning pipelines to scale their analyses. We re-frame HTE identification as a Markov-blanket discovery problem on a sufficient and aligned pre-treatment representation, and introduce Neural EXposure Interaction Search (NEXIS), an iterative procedure with provable and empirically validated consistent selection. We deploy NEXIS on two anti-poverty programs in Africa, augmenting each with satellite imagery capturing previously unmeasured environmental effect modifiers, leading to novel interpretable and prescriptive guidelines to optimize the programs’ next iterations.

TL;DR

We introduce NEXIS, a principled iterative procedure over sufficient and interpretable pre-treatment representations to identify the causal characterization of treatment effect heterogeneity — directly answering “what if, or how, should we modify our treatment assignment policy?”

Contributions

  • Causal and interpretable HTE identification
    Targeting causal and human-interpretable HTE identification, formulated as a Markov-blanket discovery problem over a sufficient and aligned pre-treatment representation. This is the first procedure to identify a causal and interpretable HTE characterization from complex observations, translating raw measurements end-to-end into practical guidelines for policy adaptation.
  • NEXIS — a principled procedure with recovery guarantees
    A novel forward-backward procedure based on conditional effect-equivalence testing, with high-probability recovery guarantees (Theorem 1). Extensive empirical validation and ablations on semi-synthetic experiments provide practical guidelines for different data regimes, covering test choice, multiple-testing correction, spectral-gap threshold, and backward elimination.
  • Two anti-poverty program deployments
    Deploying NEXIS on the Youth Opportunities Program (Uganda) and LEAP 1000 (Ghana), augmenting each with novel satellite imagery and capturing previously ignored environmental factors. The results provide concrete, prescriptive guidelines for the next iterations of these programs.

Method

From Tokens to Policy — a four-step cycle

[ Pipeline animation — coming soon ]
  1. Run a controlled experiment. Observe that the treatment effect varies across units. The key question: which pre-treatment features actually cause that variation — the direct effect modifiers Wdir?
  2. Represent complex measurements via Foundation Model + SAE. Pre-treatment observations (satellite imagery, medical imaging, …) are encoded by a frozen foundation model, then decomposed by a learned Sparse Autoencoder into a dictionary of interpretable atoms. Representation Sufficiency (SAE reconstruction) and Principal Alignment (one neuron per latent modifier) are the two key desiderata.
  3. Identify the direct effect modifiers with NEXIS. NEXIS runs a forward–backward Markov-blanket search over all candidate neurons, testing conditional CATE-equivalence at each step with Bonferroni-gated p-values. It returns the minimal and sufficient set S* with FWER ≤ α. Each selected neuron is automatically labelled by a VLM (Qwen2.5-VL-72B) contrasting top- vs. bottom-activating tiles.
    [ NEXIS forward–backward search animation — coming soon ]
  4. Update policy accordingly. The output is a causal and interpretable characterisation of who benefits most, directly actionable for program adaptation.

Experimental Power Paradox

The paradox

The failure of marginal effect-modification testing is not a small-sample artifact that more data can fix — it is structural. As experimental power grows, marginal screening converges to M* ≈ [m] and accumulates non-direct modifiers each backed by genuinely significant evidence. More power makes the gap between M* and S* more visible, not less.

In a learned SAE dictionary with m ≫ r atoms, any coordinate entangled with a real direct modifier shows marginal effect modification — not because it is causally relevant, but due to representation entanglement. Recovering S* requires conditioning — ruling out each candidate in the presence of the others. This is the design principle behind NEXIS.

Application 1 — Uganda

Youth Opportunities Program (YOP, 2008)

YOP awarded cash grants randomised across 439 groups in 331 communities in post-conflict Northern Uganda. We extend the trial with Landsat-7 imagery (2005–2007), embedded via Prithvi-EO and summarised by a 1,024-atom SAE. NEXIS selects from 170 candidates (SAE atoms, spectral indices, demographic covariates).

Outcome of interest
Skilled Employment — Key finding NEXIS returns 5 direct modifiers: 3 linguistic-group interactions (Teso, Acholi, Karamojong) and 2 satellite-derived environmental features. All follow an outside-option logic: the grant's pull into skilled trade is strongest where alternative livelihoods are weakest. Marginal screening returns 71 features from the same 170 candidates.
Log Business Assets — Key finding NEXIS returns 2 satellite-derived direct modifiers: NDVI (vegetation greenness) and structured agricultural landscape. No linguistic group survives. These environmental features capture the intensity of pre-program economic activity, moderating asset accumulation from the grant. Marginal screening returns 45 features.

Application 2 — Ghana

LEAP 1000 (LEAP, 2015–2017)

2,331 households in 162 communities across Northern/Upper East Ghana. Overall DiD ATE = +7.35 GH₡/month. We pool Landsat-8 2015 imagery (Prithvi-EO, 4,096-atom SAE) with 24 survey covariates: 155 total NEXIS candidates.

Key finding Two satellite-derived direct modifiers: ephemeral waterways and closed-canopy forest. No demographic modifier survives FWER. Active communities (6 and 5 respectively) show 6–8× the average program effect. Marginal screening returns 50+ features on the same 155 candidates.
